From Family Memories to the Perfect Holiday Plan

How I used GitHub Copilot CLI to turn years of family holiday chaos into a curated adventure for five — and saved my sanity in the process.

✏️ Anthony Sheldrake 📅 June 2025 🕐 8 min read
A
Anthony Sheldrake
Dad of three, software engineer, and self-appointed family travel planner

Chapter One

We Love a Family Holiday

There's something about packing five people into a car (or herding them through an airport) that either bonds you for life or pushes you to the edge. In our case, it's usually both — and we wouldn't have it any other way.

We're a family of five. There's me (Anthony), my wife, and our three kids: Katie (17), George (15), and Tom (8). Over the years, we've been incredibly lucky to explore some truly amazing places together — and each trip has given us memories that still come up around the dinner table years later.

One of our absolute favourite trips was Paris. It had everything: culture, food, that unmistakable buzz of a world-class city, and the sheer wonder on the kids' faces when they saw the Eiffel Tower for the first time.

Paris was magical — from the Eiffel Tower to the Arc de Triomphe, the kids loved every minute.

Chapter Two

Adventures Are What We Live For

Here's the thing about our family: we're not really a "lie on a beach for two weeks" kind of crew. Don't get me wrong — a pool day is sacred. But the holidays we remember most? They're the ones where we did something together.

Like the time we went kayaking on the Dordogne river in southern France. Picture this: crystal-clear water, medieval castles perched on limestone cliffs above us, and Tom in the front of the kayak absolutely convinced he was doing all the paddling. (He wasn't. But we let him believe it.)

Kayaking the Dordogne — castles, clear water, and a lot of splashing.

And then there was Carcassonne. If you haven't been, imagine a perfectly preserved medieval fortress city sitting on a hilltop, looking like something straight out of a storybook. George was in his element — running along the battlements, pretending to defend the castle from invaders. Tom just wanted to find the gift shop. (He always finds the gift shop.)

The medieval fortress of Carcassonne — straight out of a fairy tale.

Whether it's kayaking down the Dordogne or exploring medieval fortresses, we're happiest when we're doing something together.

Chapter Three

Even the Olympics!

2024 was something special. When we heard the Olympics were coming to Paris, we knew we had to be there. It was one of those "once in a lifetime" decisions that you make in about thirty seconds and then spend six months figuring out the logistics.

But we made it work. We watched the Taekwondo at the Grand Palais (incredible venue, incredible atmosphere), the kids collected every pin and mascot they could find, and the whole city had this electric energy that I've never experienced anywhere else.

Olympics, mascots, and — of course — a trip to Paris isn't complete without Disneyland.

And naturally, because we were in Paris with kids, we ended up at Disneyland too. Watching my wife and the younger two pose with the Sword in the Stone, wearing their Mickey ears with absolutely zero irony — that's the stuff right there. That's what holidays are about.

Chapter Four

The Planning Problem

So here's the thing. We love travelling. We've got a long list of places we want to go. But there's a problem that gets worse every single year: the planning.

When it was just two adults and a baby, planning was easy. Pick a place, book a hotel, done. But now? We've got a seventeen-year-old who wants aesthetic cafés and Instagram spots. A fifteen-year-old who wants adventure parks, go-karts, and — ideally — an F1 circuit within driving distance. And an eight-year-old whose entire holiday criteria can be summarised as: "Is there an aqua park?"

So every year, I end up with about forty-seven browser tabs open, three competing spreadsheets, a WhatsApp group full of suggestions nobody reads, and a growing sense of dread that I'm going to miss something important or book something that half the family hates.

Every year, the planning takes longer than the packing.

Seriously — I once spent an entire weekend just researching whether a specific beach in Croatia was pebbly or sandy, because Tom has opinions about pebble beaches. (He's against them. Strongly.)

I knew there had to be a better way. I just hadn't found it yet.

Chapter Five

Enter GitHub Copilot

I work in software. I use GitHub Copilot every day at my desk for coding. But it had never occurred to me to use it for something like holiday planning — until one evening when I was staring at yet another spreadsheet and thought: "What if I just… asked it?"

So I opened up GitHub Copilot CLI in my terminal and typed something like:

GitHub Copilot CLI
❯ We're a family of 5 — kids aged 17, 15, and 8.
  We want to visit Croatia in summer 2026.
  Research the best family activities — adventure,
  culture, water sports, day trips. Include costs,
  age suitability, and distances from Split.
  Build me an interactive HTML report I can share
  with my family.

# And then it just... started working.
Researching family activities in Croatia...
Analyzing 73 activities across Split, Dubrovnik, Hvar...
Building interactive HTML report...
✓ Report generated: Croatia_Activities_2026.html

I sat there watching it work and genuinely couldn't believe what was happening. It wasn't just listing a few tourist attractions. It was researching properly — checking age suitability, estimating costs, looking at distances, categorising things into adventure, culture, food, water sports. The kind of research that would have taken me an entire weekend was happening in minutes.

And the output wasn't a wall of text I'd have to wade through. It was a clean, interactive HTML page with filters, categories, and all the information structured in a way the whole family could actually browse.

So naturally, I did what any obsessive planner would do. I asked it to do the same thing for five more countries.

Chapter Six

What It Built

Over a few sessions, I worked with Copilot CLI to research and build comprehensive holiday guides for every destination on our shortlist. The results blew me away.

Six interactive reports. France's Gorges du Verdon and the Côte d'Azur. Bavaria and the German alpine lakes. Croatia's Dalmatian coast. Sicily's volcanoes and ancient ruins. Egypt's pyramids and Red Sea reefs. Austria's mountains and lakes.

Each report is a fully interactive page where you can filter activities by category, sort by cost, check age suitability, and plan out your days. It's like having a bespoke travel guide built specifically for your family.

I even got Copilot to build a showcase page to tie them all together — a central hub where the family can browse all six destinations, compare them, and start getting excited about where we're going next.

Chapter Seven

The Verdict

Let me be honest: I was sceptical at first. I've used AI tools before, and they often give you a surface-level answer that sounds confident but doesn't hold up when you dig into the details. Holiday planning felt too personal, too nuanced for a tool built for coding.

I was wrong.

This genuinely saved me weeks of research. Not days — weeks. The kind of deep, boring, repetitive research that involves opening a hundred tabs, cross-referencing prices, checking if something is suitable for an eight-year-old, and trying to figure out if that "amazing restaurant" on TripAdvisor is actually just a tourist trap.

More importantly, the reports gave the whole family a way to browse activities together. We sat around the laptop one evening, and everyone could filter to what they cared about.

Katie found her Instagram spots. George found his adventure parks. And Tom? Well, Tom found every aqua park within a hundred miles.

Is it perfect? No. You still need to book everything yourself. Some of the cost estimates need double-checking. And it occasionally suggests things that are seasonal or closed. But having all the research done, filtered for your family, and presented in a format everyone can actually use? That's a game-changer.

For the first time in years, I'm actually excited about the planning stage. And the family WhatsApp group? It's gone from "where shall we go?" arguments to "can we do THIS and THIS and THIS?" Which, as any parent knows, is a much better problem to have.
